Determinants

This module is about determinants, in particular it analyzes the case of the \(2 \times 2\) determinant.

You may recall from module 7 that if you start with a matrix \(A\) that is invertible and you apply row operations to achieve RREF then you must end up with the identity matrix. On the other hand, if you start with a matrix \(B\) that is not invertible and you apply row operations to achieve RREF then the result cannot be the identity. This is because row operations themselves do not change whether a matrix has an inverse. Therefore, since \(A\) was invertible, the RREF of \(A\) (and all of the intermediate matrices) must be invertible. Since the only invertibale RREF is the identity the identity must be the RREF of \(A\). Moreover, since \(B\) was not invertible, the RREF of \(B\) (and all of the intermediate matrices) must not be invertible. Therefore the RREF of \(B\) cannot be the identity.

The determinant is a tool that can tell us whether or not a matrix is invertible without having to compute the RREF. The determinant test is quite simple. If a matrix has a determinant of \(0\) it is not invertible. If a matrix has any other determinant it is invertible. This means that the matrix \(A\) along with its RREF and all of its intermediate matrices have a non-zero determinant, whereas matrix \(B\), its RREF and all of its intermediate matrices have a determinant of 0. Note that none of the row operations applied to \(A\) or applied to \(B\) were ever able to change whether or not the determinant was zero or non-zero.

This is a general fact: row operations do not change whether the determinant is zero or non-zero. Exactly how row operations do change the determinant is one of the topics investigated in this module. However, we also investigate how other operations such as rotations and reflections (of the columns of the matrix) change the determinant. We do these things by considering the graphical representation given by associating the determinant to the (signed) area inside the parallelogram formed by two column vectors of the matrix. In the module, this area is shown in yellow (when the determinant is positive) or orange (when the determinant is negative).

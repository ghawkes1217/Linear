Eigenvectors

This module explores the idea of projections. In particular, we consider the projection of 3-dimensional space onto a two-dimensional plane. Although, in general we can project onto any plane that passes through the origin, in this module we will only consider the following planes: the \((x,y)\)-plane, the \((x,z)\)-plane, and the \((y,z)\)-plane. Projecting onto these planes is very convenient. For instance, projecting onto the \((x,y)\)-plane is the same as just setting the \(z\)-coordinate equal to 0. In the other cases, we also only need to set the missing variable to 0. To get a geometric idea of what is going on consider the following analogy:

There is a large city with two major roads. A blue road (the \(x\)-axis) running from West to East and a green road ( the \(y\)-axis) running from South to North. Where the two roads intersect there is a red skyscraper (the \(z\)-axis). Suppose you find yourself in the following situations:

  • You are flying South to North directly over the city on your broom. Then looking down, you would see what is depicted in the leftmost diagram, that is, the \((x,y)\)-plane.
  • You are the good witch of the South. Then, walking North towards the city you would see what is depicted in the middle diagram, that is, the \((x,z)\)-plane.
  • You are the good witch of the East. Then, walking West towards the city you would see what is depicted in the right diagram, that is, the \((y,z)\)-plane.

(In the last two examples we are assuming the witches can see what lies below the ground, that is, the negative halves of the planes depicted.)

In this module you are given three vectors, one of each of the colors of the colors grey, brown, and black. You will be shown the projection of each of these vectors onto the \((x,y)\)-plane, the \((x,z)\)-plane, and the \((y,z)\)-plane. In the problems you may be allowed to move all of the vectors or just some of them. For a given vector that you are allowed to move, you may be allowed to move the vector from any of the left, middle, or right diagrams, or you may be restricted to only be able to make changes from one or two of these planes.

In the initial set-up below all three vectors begin at the origin. Try moving the grey square to move the grey vector away from the origin. You may then move the brown vector and then the black.

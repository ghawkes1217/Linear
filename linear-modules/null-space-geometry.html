Geometry of the Null Space

This module interprets the null space of a \(2 \times 3 \) matrix using a geometric framework. In particular, we consider the matrix as being represented by 3 two-dimensional vectors and the null space as the set of linear combinations of these vectors that terminate at the origin.

We start with a matrix expression in the form \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {a_{11}} & \textcolor{green}{a_{12}} & \textcolor{red}{a_{13}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} & \textcolor{green}{a_{22}} & \textcolor{red}{a_{23}} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{c_1}\\\textcolor{green}{c_2}\\\textcolor{red}{c_3} \end{bmatrix}\). From the column perspective this expression tells us to take \(\textcolor{blue}{c_1}\) times the vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {a_{11}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} \end{bmatrix} \) (this product is represented by the dotted blue vector in the diagram below) and then add to it \(\textcolor{green}{c_2}\) times the vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{green} {a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{green}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \) (represented by drawing in the dotted green vector starting at the tip of the dotted blue vector), and finally, to add to the result \(\textcolor{red}{c_3}\) times the vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {a_{13}}\\ \textcolor{red}{a_{23}} \end{bmatrix} \) (represented by drawing in the dotted red vector starting at the tip of the dotted green vector). The vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{c_1}\\\textcolor{green}{c_2}\\\textcolor{red}{c_3} \end{bmatrix}\) is in the null space of the given matrix if and only if the tip of the red vector gets us back to the origin.

For instance, in the initialized example below we have the matrix expression: \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {-1} & \textcolor{green}{1} & \textcolor{red}{0.2}\\ \textcolor{blue}{0} & \textcolor{green}{1} & \textcolor{red}{-1} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{1.5}\\ \textcolor{green}{1.3}\\ \textcolor{red}{1} \end{bmatrix} \). This expression tells to first move (in blue) 1.5 units left along the \(x\)-axis, then head diagonally up (in green) by moving 1.3 units up and 1.3 units right, and then finally (in red) 0.2 units right and 1 unit down. Since we end above the origin, we can say that the vector given in the initialized example is not in the null space of the given matrix.

If you look under the "Matrix Equation" heading, the matrix is shown on the left of the multiplication sign, (the vectors represented by its columns are shown on the graph--they are color coordinated with the columns). You can adjust the matrix in 3 different ways: by dragging the gray boxes at the ends of the vectors in the graph; by typing numerical values into the matrix; and by using the up and down arrow buttons next to the numbers (or your keyboard's arrow keys). You're also able to adjust the vector on the right side of the multiplication sign. You can do this by typing in numerical values or using the arrow buttons/arrow keys. Notice that each vector entry is color coordinated with the matrix column that it multiplies. The product of your matrix and vector is on the right side of the equation, and also shown on the graph, represented by the black dot. If this two-dimensional vector is the \(\mathbf{0}\) vector (or equivalently, if the black dot lies at the origin), then the 3-dimensional vector is in the null space of the matrix.

You will have various tasks throughout this module some of which require manipulating the matrix, some of which requiring manipulating the vector, and some that require a little of both. Sometimes, entries that you do not need to manipulate for a given question will have their adjustment controls disabled for that question.

Module 1: Linear Combinations

One of the most essential ideas in linear algebra is the idea of a linear combination of vectors. But what is a vector? Mathematically it is just a list of numbers, but in most practical applications these numbers represent things. For instance, we might represent certain properties of a solid using a vector with three entries. The first entry could represent the number of vertices, the second the number of edges, and the third the number of faces. Did you get a box from Amazon or Ebay today? How many vertices does it have? How many edges? How many faces? If you counted right you should get the vector \(\textcolor{#fc0}{\begin{bmatrix}8\\ 12\\ 6\end{bmatrix}}\). But different shapes will give different vectors: a tetrahedron results in the vector \(\textcolor{#29f}{\begin{bmatrix}4\\ 6\\ 4\end{bmatrix}}\) and an octahedron gives \(\textcolor{#c11}{\begin{bmatrix} 6 \\12\\8\end{bmatrix}}\), as shown below.

If we had a certain number of each one of these objects then the task of figuring out how many vertices, edges, and faces we have in all is just a matter of taking a linear combination of vectors. For example, if we have $5$ tetrahedrons, $7$ cubes, and $3$ octahedrons we would just compute \(5\textcolor{#29f}{\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\6\\4\end{bmatrix}} + 7\textcolor{#fc0}{\begin{bmatrix} 8 \\12\\6\end{bmatrix}} + 3\textcolor{#c11}{\begin{bmatrix} 6 \\12\\8\end{bmatrix}}\) which can be written in matrix form as \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{#29f}{4} & \textcolor{#fc0}{8} & \textcolor{#c11}{6}\\ \textcolor{#29f}{6} & \textcolor{#fc0}{12} & \textcolor{#c11}{12}\\ \textcolor{#29f}{4} & \textcolor{#fc0}{6} & \textcolor{#c11}{8} \end{bmatrix}\ \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{#29f}{5}\\ \textcolor{#fc0}{7}\\ \textcolor{#c11}{3} \end{bmatrix}\). This product equals \(\textcolor{green}{\begin{bmatrix}94\\ 150 \\ 86\end{bmatrix}}\) meaning you end up with $94$ vertices, $150$ edges, and $86$ faces.
